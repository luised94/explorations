tags: amino-acids, bonds, condensation
What type of reaction forms a peptide bond, which two functional
groups react, and what small molecule is released as a byproduct?
criteria: Condensation (dehydration) reaction. The carboxyl group (-COOH) of one amino acid reacts with the amino group (-NH2) of the next. Water (H2O) is released. The resulting C-N bond between the two residues is the peptide bond.

@@@ id: bio-enzyme-km
tags: enzyme-kinetics, michaelis-menten
after: bio-peptide-bond
What does the Michaelis constant Km represent, and what does a
low Km value indicate about an enzyme's relationship with its
substrate?
criteria: Km is the substrate concentration at which reaction velocity is half of Vmax. It is a measure of enzyme-substrate affinity. A low Km means the enzyme reaches half-maximal velocity at low substrate concentration, indicating high affinity for its substrate.

Name the four broad classifications of amino acid side chains
(R groups) by chemical property, and give one example amino acid
for each class.
criteria: (1) Nonpolar/hydrophobic -- e.g. alanine, leucine, valine. (2) Polar uncharged -- e.g. serine, threonine, glutamine. (3) Positively charged (basic) -- e.g. lysine, arginine, histidine. (4) Negatively charged (acidic) -- e.g. aspartate, glutamate.

@@@ id: bio-atp-synthesis
tags: atp, chemiosmosis, oxidative-phosphorylation
after: bio-enzyme-km
Describe the chemiosmotic mechanism by which ATP synthase produces
ATP during oxidative phosphorylation. What is the energy source
and how is it coupled to synthesis?
criteria: An electrochemical proton gradient (high H+ concentration in the intermembrane space vs. matrix) is established by the electron transport chain. Protons flow down this gradient through the Fo subunit of ATP synthase, driving rotation of the rotor. This mechanical energy is transmitted to the F1 subunit, which catalyzes phosphorylation of ADP to ATP. The proton gradient is the direct energy source.

@@@ id: bio-protein-structure
tags: protein-structure, folding
List the four levels of protein structure in order, define each
briefly, and name the bond or interaction primarily responsible
for maintaining each level.
criteria: (1) Primary -- sequence of amino acids; maintained by peptide (covalent) bonds. (2) Secondary -- local folding into alpha-helices and beta-sheets; maintained by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms. (3) Tertiary -- overall 3D fold of a single polypeptide; maintained by hydrophobic interactions, disulfide bridges, hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions among R groups. (4) Quaternary -- assembly of two or more polypeptide subunits; same non-covalent forces as tertiary plus disulfide bridges between chains
