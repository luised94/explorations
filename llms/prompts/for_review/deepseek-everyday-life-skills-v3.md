### 6. Decluttering & Home Organisation
**Prompt:**
> I'm a complete beginner at organising. My home is messy, and I don't know where to start. Give me a one-room-per-week decluttering plan that covers the whole home in 5 weeks. For each week, provide: a specific zone (e.g., kitchen, bedroom closet), a step-by-step 30-minute session structure (with timers), the 4-box method explained (keep, donate, trash, relocate), and a simple rule for deciding what stays. End with a "10-minute nightly reset" routine that keeps clutter at bay. Use plain, kind language. No KonMari jargon; assume I have average stuff and no storage system yet.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This breaks an overwhelming, infinite-seeming task into **time-boxed, single-zone missions**. The 30-minute cap uses the Pomodoro principle to bypass paralysis. The 4-box method gives every item a clear binary-ish destination, replacing dithering with action. The nightly reset turns a one-time overhaul into a sustainable rhythm, anchoring a small action to an existing cue (end of day), so the clutter never again spirals out of control.

### 7. Nutrition & Basic Meal Composition
**Prompt:**
> I know some cooking basics, but I don't know how to make a meal that's actually balanced or filling. Explain, in dead-simple terms, how to build a healthy plate using the "plate method" (half vegetables/fruit, quarter protein, quarter carbs). Then, give me 5 breakfasts, 5 lunches, and 5 dinners that follow this method, each made from common, cheap ingredients and requiring minimal steps. Include a one-page "label decoder" for the 3 most important things on a nutrition facts panel (and what to ignore). Finally, write a 3-sentence grocery mantra to use when shopping to avoid marketing traps. No diet culture, just practical nourishment.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This uses the **plate method** as a visual, rule-of-thumb framework that requires no counting, weighing, or memorising. It connects your cooking skill to the outcome of satiety and energy, not just taste. The label decoder immunises against deceptive claims by teaching pattern recognition, not encyclopaedic knowledge. The grocery mantra becomes an easily recalled filter that defends against impulse buys without needing willpower.

### 8. Paperwork & Life Admin Systems
**Prompt:**
> I have piles of mail, important documents, bills, and I'm terrified I'll lose something crucial. Design a "paper peace" system using only 5 physical folders and 3 digital folders. Explain what goes in each. Give me a 15-minute weekly admin routine (what to do in order: sort incoming, pay any bills, file, shred). Create a "vital documents" checklist (birth certificate, passport, lease, insurance, etc.) with recommended storage (physical safe, encrypted cloud, etc.). End with a script for handling a piece of scary mail (like a bill or tax notice) without freezing: what to do first, second, third.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This converts chaotic piles into a **closed-loop system** with exactly 8 containers-no room for ambiguity. The weekly 15-minute sprint prevents the buildup that creates anxiety. The vital documents checklist offloads memory to an external tool, ensuring you're covered without mental overhead. The "scary mail script" directly targets the freeze response by replacing panic with a tiny, doable sequence, making the system resilient in the face of fear.

### 9. Digital Life Management & Cybersecurity
**Prompt:**
> I'm not tech-savvy but I want my digital life to be safe and organised. Teach me, in very simple terms: (1) how to make and manage strong passwords (and what a password manager is and how to pick one), (2) how to spot a phishing email/text with 3 red flags, (3) a simple backup plan for my phone photos and important files using free or cheap tools, (4) how to unsubscribe and delete old accounts in bulk. End with a one-page "digital declutter checklist" I can do in an afternoon. Assume I use a smartphone and a laptop, and I'm easily overwhelmed by tech talk.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This treats digital hygiene as **life maintenance, not tech hobby**. Each module targets the highest-impact, lowest-effort intervention that prevents disaster (password breaches, data loss, fraud). The red-flag approach to phishing builds a quick mental heuristic rather than requiring constant vigilance. The afternoon checklist gives a concrete time window, making digital declutter feel achievable rather than infinite.

### 10. Hosting & Social Home Skills
**Prompt:**
> I never host because I'm anxious and feel my home isn't good enough. Give me a "stress-free hosting for beginners" guide. Include: (1) a shopping list of 5-8 items to always keep on hand so guests can be welcomed spontaneously, (2) a 10-minute pre-guest tidy-up checklist that makes the house feel clean even if it's not perfect, (3) 3 no-cook snack/drink ideas that look nice but require no skill, (4) a sample script to invite someone over casually, and (5) a "graceful exit" phrase to use when you're ready for guests to leave. Assume I have basic kitchenware and a small living space. Use warm, reassuring tone.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This removes the myth that hosting requires a perfect home or elaborate preparations. The "always on hand" list turns hosting from a planned production into a possibility. The 10-minute tidy concentrates effort on the visual and sensory cues (smell, sightline, surfaces) that guests actually notice. The scripts remove social anxiety by providing pre-made language, so you never have to invent the right words while nervous.

### 11. Basic DIY & Tool Literacy
**Prompt:**
> I have no tool experience but I want to be able to handle small household tasks without calling for help. Create a "first toolbox" list of 8 essential tools (under $60 total) with a one-sentence purpose for each. Then, teach me 5 foundational skills: (1) measuring and marking accurately, (2) driving a screw without stripping it, (3) using a drill to make a pilot hole and hang something on drywall with an anchor, (4) tightening loose furniture and cabinets, (5) safely cutting open a package/box without damaging the contents. For each skill, explain the exact technique, the most common wrong way, and a safety rule. End with a "5-minute tool care" habit for after each use.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This treats DIY as **physical literacy**, not handyman ambition. The 8-tool list removes the overwhelming hardware store wall. By isolating technique first (marking, driving), it prevents the frustration of ruined screws and crooked shelves that makes beginners quit. The "5-minute tool care" encodes respect for equipment as a closing ritual, extending tool life and reinforcing the new identity of someone who handles their space.

### 12. Car Basics (if applicable)
**Prompt:**
> I drive a car and know nothing about maintaining it. I want to avoid breakdowns and expensive surprises. Teach me, in simple steps: (1) how to check oil level and top it off, (2) how to check tire pressure and inflate tires at a gas station, (3) what the 5 most critical dashboard warning lights mean and what action to take for each, (4) how to jump-start a car (with a safety checklist), and (5) a seasonal maintenance reminder list (2 items per season). Include a "car emergency kit" shopping list under $30. Use language for someone who is intimidated by car guys.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This translates the opaque machine into **5 repeatable, confidence-building rituals** that prevent the most common roadside crises. Each skill is paired with a specific condition and action (warning light = what to do), so you're never left staring helplessly. The seasonal list ties checks to already-noticeable weather changes, hooking new habits onto existing cues. The emergency kit turns a vague "you should have one" into a specific purchase, making it done.

### 13. Sleep Hygiene & Bedroom Optimisation
**Prompt:**
> I have trouble sleeping and suspect my environment and habits are part of the problem. Help me redesign my sleep. Give me: (1) a checklist for making my bedroom a sleep cave (light, temperature, noise, bedding), (2) a 30-minute wind-down routine I can customise with options, (3) a "stop doing" list of 5 things that wreck sleep (and how to replace them), (4) a one-week sleep log template (simple, 3 columns). Then, write a gentle, encouraging paragraph for someone who feels broken by poor sleep. Assume I can't redesign the room from scratch, only adjust what's there.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This addresses sleep as a **learnable environmental skill**, not a moral failing. The "sleep cave" checklist gives objective, controllable variables to tweak, providing agency. A wind-down routine with options respects personal preference and low energy, making adherence possible. The "stop doing" list re-frames deprivation as a subtraction of sleep disruptors rather than adding demands. The log brings awareness without obsessive tracking, and the kind paragraph fights the shame that perpetuates insomnia.

### 14. Self-Care & Burnout Prevention for Daily Life
**Prompt:**
> I'm learning to run my home and life, but I burn out quickly and then everything falls apart. Design a "maintenance plan for the human" that goes alongside my household systems. Include: (1) a 5-minute daily self-check-in with 3 questions to gauge my state, (2) a menu of 10 micro-restorative activities (1-5 minutes) for when I'm drained but still need to function, (3) a "low mode" version of my daily rhythm (what's the absolute minimum I should do to keep life afloat on terrible days), (4) a simple boundary phrase I can use when overloaded, and (5) a one-page weekly reflection that isn't a gratitude journal-just checking in. Use language that's compassionate, not preachy.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This builds **burnout countermeasures into the operating system**, rather than treating self-care as an optional add-on. The 3-question check-in creates interoceptive awareness-catching depletion before it becomes collapse. A menu of tiny restorative actions bypasses the "I don't have time" story. The "low mode" routine explicitly gives permission to scale back without quitting, preserving momentum. The weekly reflection creates a deliberate pause that allows course correction.

### 15. Renter's Rights & Communicating With Landlords
**Prompt:**
> I rent and I feel powerless when something breaks or my landlord ignores me. Teach me my basic renter's rights and how to handle issues. Give me: (1) a 10-point bullet list of standard tenant rights (habitability, repairs, security deposit, privacy, etc.) in plain language, (2) a fill-in-the-blank template for a maintenance request email that creates a paper trail, (3) what to document when moving in and moving out (with a checklist), (4) a script for a phone call when a repair is urgent, (5) the 3 red flags that mean I should contact a tenants' rights organisation. Assume I'm in the U.S. but mention if variations apply elsewhere.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This transforms the power dynamic by **giving language and process where there was only anxiety**. The rights list is simplified to actionable knowledge, not legalese. The email template turns a vague complaint into a dated, factual record-the single most powerful tool a renter has. The move-in/out checklist prevents deposit theft. The red flags connect individual problems to outside help, so you know when the system has failed and you need advocacy.

### 16. Emergency Preparedness (Beyond First Aid)
**Prompt:**
> I have first aid basics but nothing else emergency-related. Help me prepare for common household emergencies: power outages (24-48 hours), water shutoffs, and sudden evacuation orders. Create a tailored plan: (1) a grab-and-go bag checklist (10 items max), (2) a "what to do in the first 5 minutes" flow for each scenario, (3) a home backup plan for power (flashlights, power banks, non-perishable food, water), (4) a 3-sentence family communication plan if cell towers are down, and (5) a note on pet/child preparedness. Keep it non-alarmist, just practical, and assume I live in an apartment.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This turns emergency planning from a broad, fear-inducing concept into **three specific scenario scripts**. Limiting the grab bag to 10 items prevents the "doomsday prepper" escalation trap. The "first 5 minutes" flow charts replace freeze with action-exactly what fails in a crisis. The communication plan solves the biggest source of terror (where is my person?) with a pre-agreed meeting logic. Everything is sized for an apartment dweller, making it feel relevant, not extreme.

> I want a couple of houseplants and maybe a small balcony pot or doorstep, but I'm notorious for killing everything. **Strategy verbalised:**  
This lowers the stakes to **survival-level horticulture**. Recommending only three specific, named plants removes the deadly randomness of garden-centre impulse buys. The finger test is a physical, foolproof moisture gauge. The watering lesson attacks the most common cause of death, turning a "black thumb" into a bad-habit fix. Anchoring plant care to an existing routine (Sunday reset) steals the habit momentum you've already built.

### 18. Time Awareness & Longer-Term Planning
**Prompt:**
> I can manage a daily rhythm, but I keep getting blindsided by birthdays, appointments, and tasks that require weeks of preparation. Teach me a lightweight "big picture" system. Include: (1) a monthly "calendar sweep" routine (when and how to review the upcoming 4-6 weeks), (2) a master list of recurring annual events I should pre-load into my calendar (key birthdays, tax deadlines, medical visits, seasonal home tasks), (3) a simple "backwards planning" example for a single event (like hosting a birthday dinner) so I see how to break it into tiny steps over weeks, (4) a 3-2-1 weekly planning prompt to answer every Sunday, (5) a one-page future log template (paper or digital). Assume I get overwhelmed by complex productivity systems.

**Strategy verbalised:**  
This builds a **lightweight forecasting layer** over the daily rhythm, not a heavyweight planning system. The calendar sweep is a single monthly ritual that catches incoming obligations before they become emergencies. The pre-loaded list builds a safety net so you never have to remember recurring events again. The backwards planning example teaches the invisible skill of task decomposition, and the 3-2-1 prompt creates a predictable weekly thinking slot that prevents reactive living. It's designed for the cognition of someone who can only handle one layer of planning at a time.
