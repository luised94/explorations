### 1. Grocery Shopping & Meal Planning  
*Why it completes the set:* Cooking skills are useless if your fridge is empty or full of random ingredients that don't form a meal. This is the missing bridge between "I can scramble an egg" and "I fed myself all week without wasting food or money."

**Prompt:**
> I'm a single person who hates food waste and decision fatigue. Design a repeatable weekly grocery system. Give me a master list of 20 flexible, low-waste staples (proteins, carbs, veg, pantry) that can mix into many meals. Then, create a 1-hour weekly routine: (a) a 10-minute "fridge check" template, (b) a fill-in-the-blank meal planner for 5 dinners and 2 lunches using a "cook once, eat twice" approach, (c) a categorized shopping list automatically generated from that plan. Include a "no-cook backup meal" for nights I'm exhausted. Explain the "first-in, first-out" fridge organization in one paragraph. Use normal language, no chef terms.

**Strategy verbalized:**  
This uses **systems design, not willpower**. By locking you into 20 multi-use staples, it kills the "what do I even buy?" panic. The "cook once, eat twice" rule (e.g., roast chicken tonight, chicken salad tomorrow) halves mental load. The fridge-check template and auto-generated list turn a vague chore into a step-by-step sequence. The no-cook backup meal acknowledges reality-you'll skip cooking sometimes, so having a plan prevents takeout that torpedoes your budget and health. It's a closed-loop food system for a clueless person.

### 2. Laundry & Clothing Maintenance  
*Why it completes the set:* The best capsule wardrobe falls apart if clothes are shrunken, stained, or permanently wrinkled. Laundry is a chemistry and physics skill disguised as a chore, and neglecting it erases all your dressing-well effort.

**Prompt:**
> Teach me laundry like I've never done it before. First, explain the 5 universal laundry symbols I'll actually see on tags and what they mean. Second, give me a one-page cheat sheet: which items go in cold/warm/hot water, which get hung dry, which can tumble dry low, and which must be dry-cleaned. Third, a "stain emergency" flow chart for food, oil, sweat, and red wine-what to do in the first 2 minutes. Fourth, how to wash sweaters and jeans without ruining them. Finally, a 3-step "Sunday evening reset" to keep my clothes ready all week. Assume I have a basic washer/dryer and an iron, no steamer. No marketing fluff, just action.

**Strategy verbalized:**  
This prompt treats laundry as a **protocol, not a chore**. Decoding only 5 symbols eliminates information overload. The cheat sheet replaces the paralyzing "sorting by colour" rule with clear temperature and drying instructions per fabric type-way more practical. The stain flowchart is a critical time-sensitive drill; the first 2 minutes decide whether a stain becomes permanent. Ending with a specific weekly reset ties the knowledge to a recurring calendar anchor, transforming one-time learning into a lifelong habit loop.

### 3. Basic First Aid & Home Safety  
*Why it completes the set:* Your house basics prompt covered fire extinguishers and water shut-offs, but not the human body. When you're clueless, a kitchen burn or a deep cut can turn into a panic-driven ER visit or needless infection. This gives you front-line confidence.

**Prompt:**
> I live alone and have zero medical training. Create a "home first responder" mini-guide for the 8 most common household injuries: minor burns, cuts (including when to get stitches), falls/sprains, choking, allergic reactions, splinters, kitchen knife slips, and chemical eye splash. For each: what it looks like, immediate step-by-step care, what NOT to do (common myths), and the clear signs to call 911 or go to urgent care. Then, give me a basic first-aid kit shopping list (under $30) with exactly what to buy and where to store it. Add a 1-card CPR reminder that I can print and put on the fridge. Use plain English, no medical jargon.

**Strategy verbalized:**  
This is about **reducing hesitation in the golden minute**. The clear "not to do" section busts deadly myths (butter on burns, tilting head back for nosebleeds). The cheap kit list removes the "I'll order one someday" barrier. The fridge-card CPR transforms a forgettable skill into a visual trigger; in a crisis, you don't recall lessons, you follow a placard. By linking each injury to a specific call-for-help threshold, it offloads the terrifying ambiguity of "how bad is bad?"

### 4. Simple Mending & Basic Sewing  
*Why it completes the set:* A capsule wardrobe lives and dies by buttons, hems, and small holes. Paying a tailor for every loose button or waiting until a seam splits wide open makes dressing well unsustainable. Two stitches and a bit of knowledge save hundreds of dollars and extend clothes' lives for years.

**Prompt:**
> I've never held a needle. Teach me 3 repairs I can do by hand: sew a button, fix a small ripped seam, and hem pants using iron-on tape as a no-sew option. For each, give a ridiculously simple step-by-step with what materials I need exactly (thread type, needle size, tape). Include a photo-like text description of the knot and stitches. Then, give me a "5-minute clothing check" I do before putting something in the laundry or wearing it, so I catch issues early. End with a $15 mending kit shopping list (just the essentials). Assume I don't own a sewing machine and never will.

**Strategy verbalized:**  
This strips sewing down to **survival-level repairs**-not a hobby. By limiting to three fixes, it ensures you'll actually learn them. The iron-on tape option acknowledges you're more likely to own an iron than a sewing machine, giving you a high-success first win. The pre-wear/laundry check integrates mending into existing habits (laundry day, dressing), preventing a loose thread from becoming a ruined garment. The minimal kit removes "not having the right thing" as an excuse, because the list is tiny and cheap.

### 5. Daily Rhythm & Household Time Management  
*Why it completes the set:* You can know how to cook, clean, budget, and dress, but if you have no viable daily structure, everything happens in a stressful, reactive scramble. This is the operating system that runs all the other apps.

**Prompt:**
> Help me design a realistic weekday rhythm for a single working adult who struggles with executive function. I need a morning "launch sequence" (30 min max), an evening "shutdown sequence" (20 min max), and a Sunday 1-hour weekly reset. For each block, list the non-negotiable tasks in order, with approximate times. Include gentle "if I'm really struggling, here's the bare minimum" version for each block. Then, show me how to anchor cleaning, laundry, meal planning, and a money check-in to specific days and times in this weekly rhythm, so nothing piles up. Use compassionate, non-hustle language. End with a one-page printable tracker I can mark with an X.

**Strategy verbalized:**  
This uses **habit stacking and minimum viable routines**, designed for low-executive-function days. The morning and evening sequences book-end the day with predictability, reducing cognitive load. The "bare minimum" version (e.g., "just splash water on face" instead of a full skincare routine) prevents the all-or-nothing collapse where one bad morning derails the whole week. Anchoring chores to specific days and times converts them from an infinite to-do list into recurring, time-boxed events you can trust, which is the entire secret of maintaining a home without burning out.

No single area works in isolation; they interlock to make cluelessness disappear, one small system at a time.
