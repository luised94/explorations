Here are five ready-to-use prompts, each followed by an explanation of the strategy behind it-why it's structured that way and how it helps a total beginner absorb the essentials fast.

### Prompt 1: Cooking - The 7-Day, One-Skill-a-Day Crash Course

**Prompt:**
> I am a complete beginner who has hardly cooked anything. Design a 7-day "learn to cook" crash course. Each day should focus on exactly one simple, core skill (like boiling pasta, scrambling eggs, roasting vegetables, pan-frying a chicken breast, making a one-pot meal, etc.). For each day, give me: (1) a 5-minute reading/explanation of the skill, (2) a single foolproof recipe that uses it, (3) a mini shopping list for that day's ingredients, and (4) the top 2 mistakes beginners make with that skill and how to avoid them. Assume I have only basic equipment (one pot, one pan, a baking sheet, a knife, a cutting board). At the end, give me a combined shopping list for the whole week so I can buy everything in one trip. Keep everything in plain language, no chef jargon.

**Strategy verbalized:**
This prompt uses **scaffolded skill acquisition**-it isolates one cooking technique at a time so you never face a recipe that demands five unfamiliar skills at once. The "foolproof recipe" ensures early wins (scrambled eggs are forgiving), building confidence. By giving a daily micro-lesson, it turns a giant, scary topic into a concrete, manageable routine. Asking for the top 2 mistakes preempts the exact moments beginners quit ("my pasta is mush"). The combined shopping list removes planning friction. This transforms cooking from a vague "I need to learn" into a clear, action-focussed, one-week project.

### Prompt 2: Dressing Well - The 15-Piece Capsule Wardrobe Builder

**Prompt:**
> I have no fashion sense and I want a simple, timeless, everyday wardrobe that looks put-together without effort. Create a unisex (or specify gender) 15-piece capsule wardrobe where every top goes with every bottom. List each item with a short description (fit, fabric, recommended neutral colours). For each item, explain what to look for in terms of fit and quality when shopping, and note which common mistakes scream "ill-fitting." Then, give me 10 different complete outfit combinations using only these 15 items, labelled by occasion (e.g., "casual errands," "casual office," "dinner"). Finally, write a 3-sentence "style mantra" I can repeat before I buy anything new. Assume a clean, classic look, not trendy.

**Strategy verbalized:**
This prompt leverages the **capsule wardrobe method**, which reduces infinite clothing choices to a closed system where everything matches. By explicitly asking "every top goes with every bottom," you guarantee zero fashion paralysis in the morning. Teaching what to look for in fit and fabric shifts the focus from subjective "style" to objective criteria (shoulder seams, drape, material) that a clueless person can easily check. The 10 combinations serve as training wheels-you see the logic in action. The "style mantra" condenses all advice into a memorable rule (e.g., "Fit first, neutral colours, quality over quantity"), which acts as an inner coach during shopping.

### Prompt 3: Personal Finance - The Five Principles + Fill-in-the-Blank Budget

**Prompt:**
> Act as a patient, non-judgmental money teacher. I'm starting from absolute zero. First, explain the 5 most important personal finance principles in plain, everyday language (think ELI5). For each principle, give a real-life example. Then, create a simple monthly budget template that I can literally copy and fill in. It should have clear income and expense categories suitable for a single renter with an average salary. After the template, write a "Money Fails" section: list the 3 most common financial mistakes people like me make and exactly what to do instead, with concrete steps. Finally, give me a one-sentence weekly money habit I can start this Sunday.

**Strategy verbalized:**
This prompt uses the **principle-first, tool-second** approach. It prevents overwhelm by distilling finance down to only five core ideas (like "spend less than you earn") before ever touching a spreadsheet. The provided budget template eliminates the "I don't even know what categories to list" barrier-a key reason beginners give up. Calling out specific "Money Fails" with corrective actions inoculates against predictable errors (e.g., ignoring small subscriptions, lifestyle inflation). Ending with a tiny weekly habit (like "check bank balance for 60 seconds") builds consistency by linking behaviour to an existing weekly trigger (Sunday), making the abstract tangible.

### Prompt 4: House Basics - The "Home 101" Manual & Seasonal Checklist

**Prompt:**
> I have just moved into my first unfurnished apartment and I know nothing about maintaining a home. Write me a "Home 101" manual covering these 10 essential topics: (1) circuit breaker & fuses, (2) shutting off water main, (3) plunging a toilet and unclogging a sink, (4) using a fire extinguisher, (5) changing HVAC/air filter, (6) basic wall repair (small holes), (7) locating gas shut-off, (8) hanging a shelf/curtain rod, (9) resetting a tripped disposal, (10) stopping a running toilet. For each, explain: what it's for, when you need to do it, step-by-step instructions, tools needed, and one clear warning sign to call a professional instead. After that, give me a one-page seasonal maintenance checklist (Spring, Summer, Fall, Winter) with the 3-4 tasks per season that prevent the most expensive problems.

**Strategy verbalized:**
This prompt turns an amorphous fear ("something might break") into a **curated emergency-playbook**. By pre-defining the 10 most common home moments of panic, it eliminates you not even knowing what you don't know. The structure (when, how, tools, professional warning) teaches not just steps but *decision-making*-the crucial ability to know when DIY is unsafe. The seasonal checklist shifts you from reactive panic to preventative habit. Anchoring tasks to seasons (e.g., "fall: bleed radiators, clean gutters") uses environmental cues instead of calendar dates, which are easier for a clueless person to remember.

### Prompt 5: Cleaning - The "10-Minute a Day" System & Room-Sprint Guide

**Prompt:**
> I need a cleaning routine so simple a sleep-deprived, clueless human can stick to it. Design a weekly cleaning schedule that uses only a few basic products: white vinegar, baking soda, dish soap, and a microfibre cloth. The schedule must have one quick 10-minute task per day (e.g., Monday: wipe bathroom surfaces) and a single 30-minute "power hour" on the weekend for deeper cleaning. No more. Then, create a one-page "clean any room in 15 minutes" rapid guide. It should list in order (like a checklist): what to do first, second, third-up to victory-for a living room, kitchen, bathroom, and bedroom. Add a "cleaning don'ts" box: three common mistakes that actually make things dirtier or harder (like using too much product). End with a morning "reset" ritual in 3 steps (2 minutes) to keep the house guest-ready.

**Strategy verbalized:**
This prompt weaponises **minimum viable effort** and **time-boxing**. A 10-minute daily cap bypasses the "I'll do it when I have time and energy" trap-anyone can find 10 minutes. Specifying just four cheap, non-toxic products removes the cleaning-aisle overwhelm and the belief that you need specialized sprays. The "clean any room in 15 minutes" script uses a top-to-bottom, left-to-right sequence, which prevents aimless wandering and guarantees no spot is missed. The cleaning-don'ts dispel intuitive but wrong moves (like spraying cleaner directly on the surface and letting it dry). Ending with a 2-minute reset ritual lowers the barrier to entry so low that maintenance becomes automatic, not a decision.
